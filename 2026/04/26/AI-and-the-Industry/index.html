AI and the Industry

2026/4/26

A Structural Shift

AI对绘画行业的冲击

Not long ago, the public response to AI-generated images was one of mild derision. The errors were obvious: fused fingers, incoherent textures, and compositions that fell far short of Impressionist technique. That sense of superiority has since evaporated. The pace of AI’s iterative improvement was widely underestimated. Within a remarkably short period, the technology has moved from clumsy imitation toward a capacity for displacing professional artists on a significant scale.

The underlying driver of this transition is the demand for economic efficiency. For corporations, AI offers a radical compression of production timelines. Work that once required several days of human effort can now be generated in minutes. Under these conditions, the central objective becomes the reduction of operational overhead. Volume and turnaround speed have taken precedence over the qualities historically associated with human craftsmanship. This shift should be understood not as a simple technical upgrade, but as a deliberate financial restructuring.

The resulting competitive dynamics have altered the structure of the labor market. Professionals across the industry report a wave of layoffs alongside a notable compression of wages at the entry level, as companies substitute junior staff with automated workflows. For those who retain their positions, the nature of the work has changed. Rather than originating concepts or executing pieces from the ground up, many now function primarily as operators and editors of machine-generated drafts, refining AI output to satisfy client specifications. The outcome is a market environment in which human skill is increasingly subordinated to the demands of machine throughput.

This situation exposes a fundamental contradiction within the prevailing narrative of democratization. Advocates emphasize that AI makes creative tools accessible to a broader population and relieves humans of repetitive tasks. From the perspective of working professionals, however, this accessibility carries a steep cost. Current generative models do not merely assist human effort; they circumvent the long process of skill development and cognitive engagement that defines artistic mastery. Moreover, the convenience offered by these systems rests on a legally ambiguous foundation. The models have been trained on extensive archives of human work, often without the consent or remuneration of the original creators. If a commercial entity derives financial benefit from the replication of a distinct artistic style, the originator of that style should logically be entitled to a corresponding licensing fee or royalty.

As algorithmic capacity in visual production continues to advance, it becomes necessary to examine the broader implications of this dependency. The question is no longer confined to matters of workflow or efficiency; it extends to the nature of creative agency. When stylistic choices are increasingly guided by system prompts and the collective output of past work is treated as raw material for corporate training sets, the relationship between the user and the tool begins to invert. The task ahead is to ensure that these technologies remain instruments in the service of human expression, rather than evolving into closed systems that extract value from the very communities they purport to empower.
