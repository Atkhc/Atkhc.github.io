AI and the Industry

2026/4/26

A Structural Shift

AI对绘画行业的冲击

Not long ago, the public response to AI-generated images was one of mild derision. The errors were obvious: fused fingers, incoherent textures, and compositions that fell far short of Impressionist technique. That sense of superiority has since evaporated. The pace of AI’s iterative improvement was widely underestimated. Within a remarkably short period, the technology has moved from clumsy imitation toward a capacity for displacing professional artists on a significant scale.

The underlying driver of this transition is the demand for economic efficiency. For corporations, AI offers a radical compression of production timelines. Work that once required several days of human effort can now be generated in minutes. Under these conditions, the central objective becomes the reduction of operational overhead. Volume and turnaround speed have taken precedence over the qualities historically associated with human craftsmanship. This shift should be understood not as a simple technical upgrade, but as a deliberate financial restructuring.

The resulting competitive dynamics have altered the structure of the labor market. Professionals across the industry report a wave of layoffs alongside a notable compression of wages at the entry level, as companies substitute junior staff with automated workflows. For those who retain their positions, the nature of the work has changed. Rather than originating concepts or executing pieces from the ground up, many now function primarily as operators and editors of machine-generated drafts, refining AI output to satisfy client specifications. The outcome is a market environment in which human skill is increasingly subordinated to the demands of machine throughput.

This situation exposes a fundamental contradiction within the prevailing narrative of democratization. Advocates emphasize that AI makes creative tools accessible to a broader population and relieves humans of repetitive tasks. From the perspective of working professionals, however, this accessibility carries a steep cost. Current generative models do not merely assist human effort; they circumvent the long process of skill development and cognitive engagement that defines artistic mastery. Moreover, the convenience offered by these systems rests on a legally ambiguous foundation. The models have been trained on extensive archives of human work, often without the consent or remuneration of the original creators. If a commercial entity derives financial benefit from the replication of a distinct artistic style, the originator of that style should logically be entitled to a corresponding licensing fee or royalty.

As algorithmic capacity in visual production continues to advance, it becomes necessary to examine the broader implications of this dependency. The question is no longer confined to matters of workflow or efficiency; it extends to the nature of creative agency. When stylistic choices are increasingly guided by system prompts and the collective output of past work is treated as raw material for corporate training sets, the relationship between the user and the tool begins to invert. The task ahead is to ensure that these technologies remain instruments in the service of human expression, rather than evolving into closed systems that extract value from the very communities they purport to empower.

Career Paths in the Art Industry: Geography, Roles, and Models

2026/4/26

职业路径，发展路径

In a globalized market, success is defined by navigating a complex web of trade-offs, beginning with the geographic location of one’s career.

Geographic location sets the upper boundary of opportunity. Regional ecosystems dictate the ceiling of professional growth:

Region Primary Focus Key Players Pros/Cons
North America AAA Games & Film VFX Riot, Blizzard, Disney, Sony High ceilings; mature pipelines / Visa barriers; layoffs
East Asia Mobile Games & Animation Tencent, MiHoYo, MAPPA, Nintendo High demand; fast growth / Intense competition
Europe Indie Games & Concept Art Ubisoft, CDPR, Larian Artistic diversity; better WLB / Smaller scale; lower caps

Beyond location, functional specialization becomes the next defining constraint. The choice of a specific niche shapes an artist’s daily reality and long-term trajectory.
Within major animation or game studios, artists must adhere to a “unified style,” where success is measured by the ability to synchronize with a collective vision. This is particularly true in high-volume sectors like Japanese animation or mobile game assets, where output is highly quantifiable and individual ego is secondary to brand consistency.
On the other end of the spectrum, specialized roles in environment or prop design have become increasingly technical, requiring artists to master 3D workflows and engine implementation to maintain efficiency in large-scale production.

As specialization deepens, traditional career progression is being redefined by market signals. The linear path of Junior → Senior → Lead → Art Director is no longer the sole trajectory.
Recruitment standards on platforms like LinkedIn now favor “Generalists” who can manage AI-assisted pipelines and bridge the gap between creative vision and technical implementation. The emphasis is shifting from execution alone to adaptability across workflows.

In response to these constraints, many artists turn toward personal branding as an alternative path. Unlike the “anonymous” nature of studio work, personal IP and independent illustration thrive on distinctiveness. Here, value lies in being “different” rather than “unified.” While studio artists focus on cooperation, those building a personal brand transform unique visual languages into assets with high-end premiums. However, this path demands not only artistic skill, but also the ability to navigate platforms, audiences, and market positioning.

Ultimately, career paths in the art industry are shaped less by free choice and more by layered constraints. Geography, specialization, and market demand do not operate independently; they progressively narrow the range of viable options.
In this sense, sustainability is not simply a matter of preference, but of recognizing how these constraints interact—and positioning oneself where skill, demand, and working conditions remain in a workable balance.

Systematic Self-Taught Painting

2026/4/2

From Core Logic to Professional Evolution

系统性自学绘画

Choosing a career in art today needs grand strategy and finding a practical balance between skill and survival. As Cai Xiaowan emphasized in My Career as a Father, the ability to study independently is a primary hallmark of individual achievement. In painting, for me isn’t just a grind of craftsmanship; it’s a delicate balance between a rational system and emotional expression. I believe a mature self-learner must find the equilibrium between “Internal Framework” metrics, “External Framework” strategy, and personal emotional release.

Self-taught painting shouldn’t be a blind accumulation of time. While a studio technician must master professional standards to fit the corporate, an artist responsible for their own career must split the process into a synergy between the execution layer and the management layer.
My internal framework involves a targeted research into the underlying logic of the craft—deconstructing everything into anatomy, environment, perspective, value, and composition.
I keep specific folders for my notes and match them with reference images in my library, allowing me to retrieve knowledge with surgical precision. Yet, I also leave room for “aimless” drawing. This is where I unlock creativity and alleviate anxiety; some of the most timeless works often evolve from small accidents on the page.

On the other hand, my external framework represents my awareness of the market and my own progress. Many artists fall into a “blind rush,” neglecting factors outside of technical skill and eventually hitting a professional ceiling. By breaking down large skills into small, manageable goals—like using tree diagrams for anatomy—I avoid getting lost in trivial details. I also simulate “mock exams,” conducting full-process creations to diagnose specific weaknesses. Based on these results, I list quantitative indicators for point-to-point breakthroughs.

However, the most common trap in self-study is over-systematization. While standardized paths boost efficiency, they can lead to aesthetic mediocrity or stiffness for those of us trying to develop a personal brand. Painting is my way of perceiving the world. Without a system, my expression is weak; but with only a system, the work becomes a soul-less industrial module. The breakthrough lies in using the system to build the foundation while preserving that unique, irreplaceable emotional perception.

Looking at masters like Naoki Urasawa and Mai Yoneyama, it’s clear that while they possess elite techniques, their true strength lies in their external vision. Whether it’s Yoneyama’s compression of time in a single frame or Urasawa’s observation of the “ripe” character in ordinary people, their work is a result of lifelong learning and life experience. Similarly, the journey of seniors from Gobelins proves that growing alongside difficulty is a rite of passage. In an era of infinite information, the ability to build technical depth while maintaining a strategic plan and a sensitive heart is my ultimate core competitiveness.
